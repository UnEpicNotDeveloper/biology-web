Stages of Signal Transduction

  1. Reception is the target cell’s section of a signaling molecule coming from outside the cell
  1. Receptor Protein - located at the cell’s surface and detects a signal when the signaling molecule binds to it
  2. Ligand - a molecule that binds specifically to another molecule, usually a larger one
  1. Binding of a ligand to a receptor causes a receptor protein to undergo a change in shape
  1. Types of signal molecules
  1. Large & Polar - bind to receptors on the cell’s surface which then changes shape in the cytoplasmic region
  2. Small & Nonpolar - steroids (cholesterol) and some small peptides bind to intracellular receptors (commonly then then move to the nucleus to directly regulate transcription as a transcription factor)
  3. GPCR - a signal receptor protein in the plasma membrane that responds to the binding of a signaling molecule by activating a G protein
  1. Also called a G-Protein Coupled Receptor
  1. Ligand-gated ion channel - a transmembrane protein containing a pore that opens or closes as it changes shape in response to a signaling molecule (ligand) allowing or blocking the flow of specific ions; also called a ionotropic receptor
  2. RTK - kinases become active when ligand is bound, starting phosphorylation cascade; abnormal functioning RTK is associated with breast cancer
  1. Also called a receptor tyrosine kinase
  1. Transduction is a step of series of steps that converts the signal to a form that can bring about a specific cellular response
  1. Benefits of multi-step transduction
  1. The possibility of amplifying the signal
  2. Provides more opportunities for coordination and control than simpler systems
  1. Relay molecules - often proteins, molecules that relay a signal from receptor to response
  2. Phosphorylation / Dephosphorylation
  1. Used to regulate protein activity by adding and removing phosphate groups
  1. Kinases - enzymes that transfer phosphate groups from ATP to a protein (add phosphates)
  2. Phosphatases - rapidly remove phosphate groups from proteins
  1. Second messenger - a small, nonprotein, water-soluble molecule or ion, such as cAMP that relays a signal to a cell’s interior in response to a signaling molecule
  1. Examples: small, nonprotein, water-soluble molecules or ions like Ca+2
  2. cAMP - a common intracellular signaling molecule (second messenger) in eukaryotic cells
  1. Adenylyl cyclase - an enzyme embedded in the plasma membrane that converts ATP to cAMP
  1. The transduced signal finally triggers a specific cellular response
  1. Signaling pathways lead to the regulation of one or more cellular activities, like:
  1. Regulation of protein synthesis (turning genes on or off
  1. Usually by functioning as a transcription factor
  1. Regulation of the activity of proteins, such as membrane transport proteins or enzymes
  2. Cytoskeletal changes (cell movement, exocytosis)
  3. Apoptosis (programmed cell death)
  1. Different responses among cells
  1. Different cell types have different combinations of receptors and of proteins in their transduction pathways
  2. Due to differential gene expression among cell types, this allows for cells to respond to different sets of signals in different ways and fine tune their response
